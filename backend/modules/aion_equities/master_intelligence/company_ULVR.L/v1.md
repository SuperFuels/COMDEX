1) How The Business Makes Money
Revenue drivers:
Units sold (volume) times average selling price (price/mix) across four business groups — Beauty & Wellbeing, Personal Care, Home Care, Foods. Reported in euros but approximately 59% generated in emerging market currencies. Volume growth is the structurally preferred driver — price growth without volume is a warning sign of consumers trading down or markets shrinking.
Margin drivers:
Gross margin is the primary lever — driven by input cost movements (palm oil, petrochemicals, packaging), volume leverage on fixed manufacturing costs, and premium mix shift. Overhead efficiency is secondary lever — productivity programme delivers structural savings. Brand and marketing investment (BMI) is a deliberate cost — management targets approximately 16% of turnover and trades this off against margin. Underlying operating margin is the headline management metric — currently 20.0%.
Cash conversion drivers:
Working capital discipline — Unilever targets 100% cash conversion (FCF as percentage of net profit excluding disposals). Trade payable days management is a consistent lever. Tax on disposals is a recurring distortion — strip this out when reading FCF. Capital expenditure is approximately €1.4-1.6bn annually — relatively stable.
Capital allocation drivers:
Priority order consistently stated: invest in growth and productivity first, portfolio reshaping second, dividend third, buybacks from surplus cash. Quarterly dividend is a commitment — has not been cut. Buybacks are discretionary — signal management confidence in cash generation. Portfolio disposals are accelerating — Foods non-core brands being exited, Beauty and Personal Care being acquired into.

2) Permanent Drivers
Positive Drivers (recurring)

Emerging market volume growth, especially India and Indonesia — these markets have structural consumption growth and Unilever has deep distribution moats built over decades
EUR weakening against BRL, INR, IDR, USD — FX tailwind on reported numbers even when underlying performance is flat
Commodity input cost deflation — palm oil, crude oil derivatives, packaging — directly expands gross margin without pricing action required
Premium mix shift within categories — higher ASP units growing faster than base, positive UPG with positive UVG simultaneously
India specifically accelerating — Hindustan Unilever is the canary, reports 6 weeks before group, directly predictive
Productivity programme savings delivery — structural overhead reduction, not cyclical
China recovery — flat or positive after extended decline is a significant positive given 4% of group and management investment in reset

Negative Drivers (recurring)

EUR strengthening against EM currencies — the single most consistent drag on reported numbers, especially BRL and INR
Commodity input cost inflation — palm oil spikes pass through to COGS with approximately one quarter lag
Developed market volume declines — Europe and North America consumer confidence weakening hits the 41% developed markets exposure
Latin America macro deterioration — Brazil and Mexico together are the largest EM currency exposure and most volatile
Management language shifting from volume-led to price-led — signals underlying demand weakness being masked by pricing
Foods segment deterioration — lowest growth, lowest strategic priority, but still 26% of turnover — declining developed market Foods drags group
Indonesia or China operational reset costs — historically these markets have required periodic corrections that depress margins temporarily


3) Exposure Map
3.1 FX Exposure
Reporting currency: Euro (EUR)
EUR/BRL — Latin America approximately 37% of group
Brazil is the single largest emerging market by revenue. BRL is historically volatile — depreciated 9.3% against EUR in 2025. Mechanism: Unilever Brazil generates revenues in BRL, translates to EUR at average quarterly rate for reporting. When BRL weakens 10% against EUR approximately 3.5% of group revenue is worth 10% less in reported terms — approximately 0.35% drag on reported group turnover per 1% BRL depreciation given weighting. 2025 average rate 6.297 versus 2024 average 5.761.
EUR/INR — India approximately 12-15% of group and growing
Hindustan Unilever is the listed subsidiary — its results are published approximately 6 weeks before Unilever group results, making INR one of the highest-value leading indicators. INR depreciated 7.7% in 2025. Sensitivity approximately 0.15% drag on group reported turnover per 1% INR depreciation.
EUR/IDR — Indonesia approximately 6-8% of group
More volatile than INR. IDR depreciated 7.6% in 2025. Lower weight than India but management flagged Indonesia as a key reset market — operational improvement in H2 2025 was visible in IDR-denominated results before group reported.
EUR/USD — North America approximately 22% of group
USD depreciated 3.6% against EUR in 2025. North America is the highest-margin geography. Sensitivity approximately 0.20% drag on group reported turnover per 1% USD depreciation. EUR/USD also affects dollar-denominated commodity input costs — a stronger dollar increases Unilever's commodity costs even for non-US operations.
EUR/CNY — China approximately 4% of group
CNY depreciated 4.4% in 2025. Lower direct revenue weight but China is a strategic priority market — management attention disproportionate to current revenue share.
EUR/TRY — Turkey small but hyperinflationary
TRY depreciated 34% in 2025. Hyperinflation accounting treatment (IAS 29) means Turkey is excluded from underlying price growth above 26%. Net impact on reported numbers is complex and partially offset by hyperinflation accounting adjustments.
Composite FX basket calculation method:
Weight each currency pair by its geography's share of group turnover. Apply the percentage change in quarterly average rate versus prior year same quarter. Sum weighted impacts to get total FX drag or tailwind on reported turnover.
Composite FX impact = 
  (LatAm weight 0.37 × EUR/BRL % change) +
  (India weight 0.13 × EUR/INR % change) +
  (NA weight 0.22 × EUR/USD % change) +
  (Indonesia weight 0.07 × EUR/IDR % change) +
  (China weight 0.04 × EUR/CNY % change) +
  (Other EM residual 0.17 × blended EM rate % change)
Update daily using ECB reference rates. Compute running quarterly average versus prior year same quarter average. This is the pre-announcement currency impact estimate — zero lag, observable every day.
3.2 Commodity and Input Cost Exposure
Key inputs identified across 8 quarters:

Palm oil — primary input for Home Care (fabric cleaning, dish cleaning) and some Personal Care. Globally traded, futures available on Bursa Malaysia and CME.
Petrochemical derivatives — packaging materials, surfactants for cleaning products. Proxy: crude oil price, ethylene spot price.
Packaging — cardboard, plastic, aluminium. Proxy: container board index, aluminium futures.
Agricultural commodities — wheat, soy derivatives for Foods segment. Less material than cleaning/personal care inputs.

Public proxies:

Palm oil: Bursa Malaysia Crude Palm Oil Futures (FCPO) — daily
Crude oil: Brent crude — daily
Container freight: Freightos Baltic Index — weekly
Aluminium: LME aluminium — daily

Which line items they hit:
Input cost changes hit cost of goods sold directly. Gross margin is the first line impacted. Typically 1-2 quarter lag between spot price movement and reported gross margin impact because Unilever hedges forward. When spot prices move management will comment on "commodity tailwinds" or "commodity headwinds" in the following 1-2 quarters of results.

4) Management Language Baseline
Repeated phrases across 8 quarters (top 5):

"Volume-led growth" — appears when management is confident in demand. Absence or qualification is a warning signal.
"Sharper focus" / "simpler, sharper, faster" — Fernando Fernandez era language. Signals strategic confidence.
"Disciplined execution" — paired with margin delivery. When this phrase weakens it typically precedes a cost miss.
"Ahead of plan" / "ahead of schedule" — used specifically for productivity programme. Credible — they have consistently delivered on this.
"Slowing markets" — appeared 3 times in Q4 2025 results. New cautionary framing that was not present in Q2/Q3 2025. Watch for whether this persists or resolves.

Confidence markers (bullish signals in language):

"Accelerating" applied to volume growth
"Strong" applied to specific market or category without qualification
Tight guidance range (e.g. "within 4-6%") versus wide range
Specific country called out by name with positive trajectory
Productivity programme delivery described as "ahead of plan"

Hedge and weakness markers (bearish signals):

"Despite" — signals a positive result in a difficult context, implies fragility
"Although" — same function
"Slowing markets" — macro cover for weaker underlying
"Soft comparators" benefiting growth — means prior period was weak, current growth is partly base effect
"Early progress" on corrective actions — means it's not fixed yet
"Challenging consumer environment" — emerging market demand weakness
Guidance qualified with "at the bottom end" — as in Q4 2025: "bottom end of 4-6% range"

Topics they obsess over (always present):

Volume versus price decomposition — management knows the market watches this
Emerging market recovery narrative — India, Indonesia, China always mentioned
Productivity programme savings progress — specific euro amounts always cited
Brand and marketing investment levels — always expressed as percentage of turnover
Portfolio transformation progress — number of transactions completed

Topics that disappear before misses (historically):

Specific country growth called out positively — when a market stops being named it is usually declining
"Market share gains" language — when this disappears volume is being supported by price not by winning consumers
Specific innovation names cited — when flagship innovations stop being mentioned they are underperforming

## 6) What Good Looks Like

**Volume-price mix:**
USG above 4.5% with UVG above 2.0% and UPG above 2.0% simultaneously. This balanced mix has historically been the condition under which Unilever's stock re-rates. Pure price growth without volume at or above 1.5% is not a re-rating condition.

**Geographic balance:**
Asia Pacific Africa growing above 5% USG, Americas growing above 3% USG, Europe growing above 1.5% USG. When all three regions are positive simultaneously — which is rare — the stock has historically re-rated 15-25% over the following 4-6 quarters.

**Margin trajectory:**
Underlying operating margin expanding year on year with gross margin contribution positive. When productivity savings are delivering AND gross margin is expanding simultaneously the operating leverage is highest.

**FX environment:**
Composite FX impact better than minus 2% versus prior year. When FX impact improves from the minus 5-6% range to minus 2% or better the reported numbers start to reflect underlying performance and consensus estimates typically need to be revised upward.

**Language confirmation:**
CEO uses "volume-led growth" without qualification, names specific markets with positive momentum, gives tight guidance range, describes productivity programme as ahead of plan.

**Re-rating confirmation:**
Two consecutive quarters of USG above 4.5% with positive volume, improving FX composite, margin expansion, and HUL results confirming India strength. This combination has historically been sufficient for the market to re-rate Unilever from a yield stock to a growth stock — the multiple expansion from that transition is the primary return driver.

## 7) What Bad Looks Like

**Volume stall:**
Group UVG below 1.0% for two consecutive quarters. This is the thesis-breaking condition. Unilever is not a price-growth story — if volume stalls the premium valuation is not supportable.

**FX intensification:**
Composite FX impact worse than minus 7%. At this level even strong underlying growth cannot offset the translation drag and reported turnover declines become a narrative problem regardless of underlying performance.

**Commodity spike:**
Palm oil above USD 1,200 per tonne sustained for two quarters. At this level gross margin compression typically exceeds 100 basis points and management is forced into pricing action that risks volume.

**Latin America deterioration:**
Brazil and Mexico both declining simultaneously. This has happened before and is typically associated with broader EM risk-off sentiment that also weakens BRL, creating a double negative — volume down and FX headwind simultaneously.

**Management credibility break:**
Guidance missed for two consecutive periods. Productivity programme delivery qualification. Language shifting from specific to vague on market recovery narratives.

**Foods acceleration failure:**
Foods is the least strategically important segment but at 26% of turnover a sustained decline drags the group. If Foods USG goes negative while Beauty and Personal Care are growing the mixed picture creates valuation uncertainty.

## 8) Sensitivity Coefficients

**EUR/BRL — 1% BRL depreciation against EUR:**
Approximately 0.37% drag on reported group turnover, approximately 0.15% drag on reported underlying operating profit translated to euros. Approximately 0.04% drag on underlying EPS.

**EUR/INR — 1% INR depreciation against EUR:**
Approximately 0.13% drag on reported group turnover. India UOP margin is above group average so EPS impact slightly higher proportionally.

**Group UVG — 1 percentage point additional volume growth:**
Approximately 0.4-0.5 percentage points additional underlying operating margin due to fixed cost leverage. Based on 2023-2025 observed relationship between volume and margin.

**Palm oil — 10% increase in FCPO price:**
Approximately 20-30 basis points gross margin headwind with 1-2 quarter lag. Based on management commentary across 6 quarters of cost disclosure.

**Productivity programme — full €800m delivered:**
Approximately 160 basis points overhead improvement versus a business without the programme. The incremental €130m remaining in 2026 represents approximately 26 basis points.

**Composite FX — 1 percentage point improvement in basket:**
Approximately 0.5 percentage points improvement in reported turnover growth rate. The relationship is roughly linear within the normal range of FX movements.
